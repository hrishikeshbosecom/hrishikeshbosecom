## Reasoning Tools in Mathematics
A more complete map of reasoning tools:
1. Formal Logic (rules of valid inference)
	•	Propositional logic
	•	Predicate logic (first-order, higher-order)
	•	Modal logic (possibility, necessity, knowledge, time)
	•	Fuzzy logic (degrees of truth)
	•	Non-classical logics (intuitionistic, paraconsistent)
2. Mathematics (structure and quantity)
	•	Algebra (linear, abstract, Boolean)
	•	Calculus (differential, integral, vector)
	•	Geometry & topology
	•	Number theory
	•	Discrete mathematics (combinatorics, graph theory)
3. Probabilistic & Statistical Reasoning (uncertainty)
	•	Probability theory
	•	Statistics (frequentist, Bayesian)
	•	Information theory (entropy, mutual information)
	•	Decision theory (choices under uncertainty)
4. Computational Reasoning (algorithms and complexity)
	•	Algorithms & data structures
	•	Complexity theory (what’s solvable, how efficiently)
	•	Computability theory (what’s solvable at all)
	•	Type theory
5. Scientific Reasoning (empirical methods)
	•	Hypothesis formation & falsification
	•	Experimental design
	•	Causal inference (correlation ≠ causation, confounders)
	•	Model building & validation
6. Analogical & Conceptual Reasoning
	•	Analogy (mapping structure from known to unknown)
	•	Abstraction (extracting general patterns)
	•	Categorization & taxonomy
	•	Conceptual modeling
7. Argumentation & Dialectic (informal but rigorous)
	•	Argument structure (claims, evidence, warrants)
	•	Identifying fallacies
	•	Steelmanning opposing views
	•	Socratic questioning
8. Heuristic Reasoning (rules of thumb when formal tools fail)
	•	Occam’s razor (prefer simpler explanations)
	•	Fermi estimation (back-of-envelope calculation)
	•	Base rates & priors
	•	Inversion (think about what would make you wrong)
9. Systems Thinking
	•	Feedback loops
	•	Emergence (whole ≠ sum of parts)
	•	Network effects
	•	Equilibria & dynamics
10. Causal Reasoning (a special category that’s gained prominence)
	•	Counterfactuals (“what would have happened if…”)
	•	Causal graphs (Judea Pearl’s work)
	•	Mechanism identification
A useful way to organize this:



|Category         |Handles             |Example question                  |
|:----------------|:-------------------|:---------------------------------|
|Logic            |Certainty, deduction|“Does B follow from A?”           |
|Math             |Structure, quantity |“How much, how fast, what shape?” |
|Probability/Stats|Uncertainty         |“How likely, how confident?”      |
|Computation      |Process, feasibility|“Can it be done, how efficiently?”|
|Scientific method|Empirical truth     |“Is this true in reality?”        |
|Causal           |Cause-effect        |“Why did this happen?”            |
|Analogical       |Pattern transfer    |“What is this like?”              |
|Heuristic        |Quick judgment      |“What’s the best guess?”          |
|Systems          |Interactions        |“How do parts affect the whole?”  |
|Argumentation    |Persuasion, debate  |“Is this reasoning sound?”        |

Two important refinements to your list:
	1.	Statistics and probability are related but distinct. Probability is the math of uncertainty (forward: given a model, predict data). Statistics is the inverse (given data, infer the model). Both matter.
	2.	Algebra is a huge family, not one thing. Linear algebra (vectors, matrices) is foundational for ML and physics. Abstract algebra (groups, rings) underlies cryptography and symmetry. Boolean algebra connects back to your propositional logic.
The deeper insight:
Most real problems need multiple tools combined. A clinical trial uses:
	•	Statistics (is the effect significant?)
	•	Causal reasoning (does the drug cause the improvement?)
	•	Logic (does the conclusion follow?)
	•	Systems thinking (side effects, interactions)
	•	Scientific method (is it reproducible?)
So rather than memorizing a list, develop the meta-skill of asking:
What kind of question am I really asking? Which tool fits?
That’s the actual reasoning skill — knowing which tool to reach for. The tools themselves are just instruments; the wisdom is in selecting and combining them. 🧠
Your textbook journey through discrete math (propositional → predicate → sets → graphs → combinatorics → probability) is actually a tour through several of these categories. By the end you’ll have a solid foundation in the “formal” side of this map. The empirical and heuristic sides come from other disciplines and from practice.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
