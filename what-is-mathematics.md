# What is Mathematics?

## Core Definition

Mathematics is the study of **abstract structures and the relationships between them** using **deductive reasoning from axioms**.

Its defining feature: conclusions are *proven*, not observed or measured.

---

## What Counts as Pure Mathematics

The following are all subfields of mathematics:

- Logic (propositional, predicate)
- Algebra (linear, abstract, Boolean)
- Calculus (differential, integral, vector)
- Geometry & topology
- Number theory
- Discrete mathematics & graph theory
- Probability theory (the *math* of it)

---

## What is NOT (Purely) Mathematics

### 1. Statistics (the applied side)
- The math of probability is mathematics
- But *doing* statistics — choosing which test, interpreting p-values, dealing with messy real data — is a craft that uses math but isn't reducible to it
- Two statisticians can look at the same data and disagree, which doesn't happen in pure math

### 2. Scientific Method
- Hypothesis testing, experimental design, falsifiability — these are **epistemological** practices
- They use math heavily, but the core ideas (Popper's falsifiability, Kuhn's paradigms) are philosophy of science

### 3. Causal Reasoning
- Judea Pearl built mathematical tools for causality, but the *concept* of cause-and-effect is metaphysical
- "Correlation ≠ causation" isn't a theorem; it's a methodological insight

### 4. Analogical Reasoning
- "X is like Y" — this is cognitive, not mathematical
- Mathematics can model it (category theory tries), but the skill itself is pattern recognition

### 5. Heuristic Reasoning
- Occam's razor isn't a theorem (though there are math-flavored versions like minimum description length)
- Fermi estimation uses math, but the skill is *judgment* about which approximations to make

### 6. Argumentation & Dialectic
- Identifying a fallacy, steelmanning an opponent — these are rhetorical and philosophical skills
- Math can formalize *some* of this (formal logic), but persuasion and debate exceed it

### 7. Systems Thinking
- Some parts are mathematical (dynamical systems, network theory)
- But concepts like "emergence" remain philosophically contested

### 8. Computational Thinking
- The theory (computability, complexity) is mathematics
- But programming as a *practice* — naming, debugging, designing for humans — is engineering and craft

---

## The Clean Distinction

| Mathematics | Not (Purely) Mathematics |
|:------------|:-------------------------|
| Proves things from axioms | Argues from evidence |
| Deals with abstract objects | Deals with real-world phenomena |
| Truth is certain (within axioms) | Truth is provisional |
| No experiments needed | Experiments required |
| Disagreement is rare and resolvable | Disagreement is normal |

---

## A More Accurate Statement

> *Mathematics provides the formal backbone for many reasoning tools, but reasoning itself is broader than mathematics. Reasoning includes empirical, interpretive, and judgment-based skills that mathematics alone can't capture.*

---

## An Analogy

**Mathematics is to reasoning what grammar is to communication.**

- Grammar gives you the rules for well-formed sentences
- But communication also requires vocabulary, context, tone, audience awareness, cultural sensitivity
- A grammatically perfect sentence can still fail to communicate
- Conversely, broken grammar can still convey meaning

Similarly, mathematics gives you the rules for valid inference. But real reasoning also requires:

- Knowing which axioms to adopt
- When a formal model fits reality
- How to act under irreducible uncertainty
- How to weigh values

None of which mathematics alone decides for you.

---

## The Wigner Insight

Eugene Wigner wrote a famous essay titled *"The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics in the Natural Sciences."*

The surprise being that math works *so well* for describing physical reality. The "unreasonable" part is the giveaway: there's no logical guarantee that abstract math should describe the messy real world.

That bridge between math and reality is **not itself mathematics** — it's something else (philosophy, science, intuition).

---

## Summary

- **Math** = the formal language of certain reasoning
- **Reasoning** = math + empirical judgment + interpretation + values + craft

Math is *central* to formal reasoning. But reasoning extends beyond math wherever the real world's messiness, uncertainty, or meaning enters the picture.

This is why fields like statistics, economics, and computer science are sometimes called **"mathematical sciences"** rather than just "mathematics" — they sit on the boundary, using math as a tool but adding empirical and practical dimensions math alone doesn't provide.
